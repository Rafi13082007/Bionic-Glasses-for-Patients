Researching sleep biometrics.

Our vision is to create an all inclusive, science backed sleep biometrics wearable.

CAD render of concept MemorySight wearable

Problem

  1. Sleep is valuable
  2. There is not enough personalised medicine technology to optimise sleep
  3. As the prevalence of sleep disorders is increasing, new methods for ambulatory sleep measurement are required. 

Despite the growing popularity and accessibility of sleep tracking technologies, current consumer-grade devices and applications lack the scientific rigor necessary to provide accurate and reliable data. Most sleep trackers rely on movement and heart rate sensors to estimate sleep stages, but these methods are inherently limited in precision and often fail to account for individual variations in sleep patterns. Additionally, the algorithms used to interpret data from these sensors are typically proprietary and not subject to rigorous peer review, leading to potential biases and inaccuracies. This lack of scientific validation and standardization undermines the effectiveness of sleep tracking as a tool for improving sleep health, potentially leading to misguided decisions based on inaccurate data. Addressing these shortcomings requires a concerted effort to incorporate validated scientific methods, increase transparency in algorithm development, and standardize measurement protocols across devices.

Solution

A wearable which tracks three main biometrics:

Heart Rate Variability

Skin conductance (sweat gland activity)

Blood Pressure Variability

1. HRV and sleep quality are linked

A 2020 research study (cited left) concluded that poor sleep quality are adversely associated with HRV, HR and BP. “Higher PSQI score correlated with lower SDANN rise (OR=0.92). Fairly bad to very good subjective sleep quality had association with lower SDANN (OR=0.43). Very high sleep latency to very low sleep latency ratio had association with lower SDANN (OR=0.39) and lower PNN50 (OR= 0.44). Sleep duration and HRV parameters had no significant association. Fairly bad sleep efficiency to very good sleep efficiency ratio was correlated with lower SDANN (OR= 0.29). Very high daytime dysfunction to very low daytime dysfunction ratio had correlation with lower SDANN (OR=0.35). Very bad compared to very good subjective sleep quality had significant correlation with higher Heart rate (HR) (B=0.03). Very high sleep latency compared to no sleep latency was associated with higher HR (B=4.74). Very high compared to very low amount of sleep disturbances correlated with higher SBP levels (B=15.2). Using sleep medication less than once a week compared with no history of taking such drugs was associated with higher HR (B=16.4).”

Heart rate variability (HRV), a measure of the variation in time between each heartbeat, is closely linked to sleep quality. Higher HRV is generally associated with better sleep quality, reflecting a well-functioning autonomic nervous system capable of effectively managing stress and recovery. During restorative sleep stages, such as deep sleep and REM sleep, HRV tends to increase, indicating a state of relaxation and efficient physiological recovery. Conversely, lower HRV can signal poor sleep quality and heightened stress, potentially stemming from disturbances like sleep apnea or insomnia. Monitoring HRV provides valuable insights into an individual’s sleep patterns and overall health, helping identify and address sleep-related issues.

2. Blood Pressure Dipping is linked to sleep variability and irregularity

Association Between Blood Pressure Variability With Dementia and Cognitive Impairment: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

Sleep variability and sleep irregularity are associated with blunted BP dipping in the general population.

 A 1-hour increase in the SD of sleep midpoint was associated with a 1.16% decrease in nighttime BP dipping (P<0.001). A 1-hour increase in the SD of sleep duration was associated with a 1.39% decrease in nighttime BP dipping (P=0.017).

3. Skin conductance can be used for identifying sleep stages and monitoring sleep

“Analysis of variance indicated significant EDASEF differences between the sleep stages wake versus N1, wake versus N2, wake versus slow-wave sleep, and wake versus rapid eye movement. The analysis of EDAcounts also showed significant differences, especially in the stages slow-wave sleep versus rapid eye movement. Between healthy subjects and patients, a significant disparity of EDAcounts was revealed in stage N1. Furthermore, the variances of EDASEF and EDAcounts in N1, N2 slow-wave sleep and rapid eye movement were higher in the patient group (p [F test] < .05).”

The results of this study indicate that electrodermal activity is not only a robust parameter for describing sleep, but also a potential suitable method for ambulatory sleep monitoring.
